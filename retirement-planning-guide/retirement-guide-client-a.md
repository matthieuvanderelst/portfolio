## The five risks that matter most in your first five years

Most retirement anxiety is not about money. It is about not knowing what the risks actually are. Here they are, named clearly.

---

### Risk 1 — Sequence of returns risk

**What it is:** If the market drops significantly in your first two to three years of retirement, the combination of withdrawals plus losses can permanently damage your portfolio — even if the market fully recovers later.

**Why it matters:** A 30% drop in year one of retirement has a fundamentally different impact than the same drop in year ten. Early withdrawals lock in losses.

**What to do about it:** Keep 12-24 months of living expenses in cash or short-term bonds before you retire. This is your buffer. You draw from it during downturns instead of selling investments at a loss.

---

### Risk 2 — Longevity risk

**What it is:** Living longer than your money lasts. The average 62-year-old woman lives to 87. Many live to 95 or beyond.

**Why it matters:** A 25-30 year retirement is now the planning assumption, not the exception.

**What to do about it:** Plan for age 90-95 as your baseline. Delay Social Security if possible — every year you wait past 62 increases your benefit. The difference between claiming at 62 versus 70 can be 75% or more in monthly income.

---

### Risk 3 — Healthcare cost risk

**What it is:** Healthcare is one of the largest and least predictable expenses in retirement. The average couple retiring today will spend $300,000+ on healthcare over their retirement years.

**Why it matters:** Medicare does not cover everything. Long-term care (assisted living, in-home care) is rarely covered at all.

**What to do about it:** Budget a dedicated healthcare line item in your retirement income plan. Consider a supplemental Medicare policy. Review long-term care insurance options before age 65 when premiums are lower.

---

### Risk 4 — Inflation risk

**What it is:** Even modest inflation of 3% per year cuts your purchasing power in half over 24 years.

**Why it matters:** A retirement income that feels comfortable today may feel tight in 15 years.

**What to do about it:** Keep a portion of your portfolio in equities throughout retirement, not just until you retire. The common mistake is moving entirely to bonds and cash too early. You need some growth assets to outpace inflation.

---

### Risk 5 — Unexpected expense risk

**What it is:** Home repairs, family emergencies, medical events, or other large one-time costs that your regular income cannot absorb.

**Why it matters:** A single $40,000-$80,000 unplanned expense can be destabilizing if you have no reserve and have to liquidate investments at the wrong time.

**What to do about it:** Build and maintain a 3-year cash reserve (see below). This is not your emergency fund — it is a strategic buffer that protects your investment portfolio from forced withdrawals.

## The three-bucket strategy

Rather than treating your retirement assets as one pool, divide them into three buckets with different jobs.

| Bucket | What it holds | Job | Time horizon |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bucket 1 — Cash** | HYSA, money market, short-term T-bills | Cover 12-24 months of expenses. Never invest this. | 0-2 years |
| **Bucket 2 — Income** | Bonds, dividend stocks, conservative funds | Generate income to refill Bucket 1 | 2-10 years |
| **Bucket 3 — Growth** | Broad index funds (QQQM, VTI, VOO) | Grow to fight inflation and fund late-stage retirement | 10+ years |

**How withdrawals work:**
1. Take income from Bucket 1 first
2. When Bucket 1 runs low, refill it from Bucket 2 income and dividends
3. Rebalance Bucket 3 into Bucket 2 every 2-3 years in good market years
4. In bad market years, leave Bucket 3 alone and live off Bucket 1 and 2

This strategy means you never have to sell growth investments at a loss to pay for groceries.

## Social Security timing

You can claim as early as age 62, or as late as age 70. Every year you wait after 62 increases your monthly benefit.

| Claim age | Approximate benefit multiplier |
|---|---|
| 62 | 70% of full benefit |
| 65 | 86% of full benefit |
| 67 (full retirement age) | 100% of full benefit |
| 70 | 124% of full benefit |

**The key question:** Do you have enough in Bucket 1 and Bucket 2 to cover the gap years if you wait to claim?

If yes, waiting is almost always the better mathematical choice — especially for women, who statistically live longer and benefit more from the higher lifetime payment.

This is not a mandate to wait until 70. It is a tradeoff: take less now and guarantee more later, or take more now and have less guaranteed for your later years.

## Understanding RMDs (Required Minimum Distributions)

Starting at age 73, the IRS requires you to withdraw a minimum amount from traditional IRAs and 401(k)s each year and pay income tax on it.

**Why this matters for planning:** If you have a large traditional IRA and do not do any planning before 73, the forced withdrawals can push you into a higher tax bracket.

**What to consider:**
- In the gap years between retirement and 73, consider doing Roth conversions — moving money from a traditional IRA to a Roth IRA and paying tax now at a lower rate, before RMDs force larger withdrawals at a potentially higher rate later
- A tax advisor can model the optimal conversion amount each year based on your bracket

## Your 3-year reserve calculator

Use the slider below to model your reserve target.

**Step 1:** Estimate your monthly expenses in retirement.

**Step 2:** Subtract guaranteed monthly income (Social Security, pension, rental income).

**Step 3:** Multiply the gap by 36 (3 years). That is your target Bucket 1 reserve.

*Example: $5,000/month expenses minus $2,000/month Social Security = $3,000/month gap × 36 = $108,000 reserve target.*

This reserve sits in a high-yield savings account (HYSA) or short-term Treasury bills — not invested in the market.

## Next steps

1. Get a Social Security statement at ssa.gov/myaccount — this shows your estimated benefit at 62, 67, and 70
2. List all guaranteed income sources and their start dates
3. Estimate monthly expenses in retirement (use current spending as a baseline and adjust for no mortgage, healthcare additions, and discretionary changes)
4. Calculate your Bucket 1 reserve target
5. Schedule a conversation with a fee-only financial advisor (not commission-based) to model the full picture
